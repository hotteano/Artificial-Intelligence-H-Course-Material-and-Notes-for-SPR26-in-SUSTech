\begin{abstract}
Information Exposure Maximization (IEM) aims to balance the spread of competing campaigns and mitigate information disparity in social networks. Driven by the NP-hard nature of the diffusion models, we empirically investigate two distinct approaches relying on Monte Carlo (MC) sampling: an MC-SAA Greedy Heuristic with IMRank candidate screening, and a Binary Evolutionary Algorithm with Simulated Annealing refinement. Rather than performing a strict performance comparison between the two—given their differing scalability constraints and dataset configurations—this study focuses on characterizing their individual behaviors under various graph topologies and propagation probabilities. Furthermore, we conduct a cross-architecture hardware benchmark to reveal how algorithmic bottlenecks respond to modern CPU upgrades. Our findings underscore that computational efficiency is dominated not merely by graph size, but by the effective diffusion range dictated by transmission probabilities.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Information Exposure Maximization (IEM) studies how to reduce information disparity between two competing campaigns over a directed social network. Given initial seed sets $I_1$ and $I_2$, we select additional seed sets $S_1$ and $S_2$ under a total budget constraint to maximize balanced exposure under Independent Cascade (IC) propagation.

Let $R_1$ and $R_2$ denote the reached node sets of campaign 1 and campaign 2 in one diffusion realization. The optimization target is
\begin{equation}
\Phi = \left|V - (R_1 \oplus R_2)\right| = |V| - |R_1 \oplus R_2|,
\end{equation}
where $\oplus$ is symmetric difference. The expected objective is
\begin{equation}
\max_{S_1,S_2} \; \mathbb{E}[\Phi],
\quad \text{s.t. } |S_1|+|S_2|\le k,\; S_1\cap S_2=\varnothing,
\end{equation}
with full seeds $I_1\cup S_1$ and $I_2\cup S_2$.

This report implements and compares three core algorithmic components:
\begin{itemize}
        \item Monte Carlo IC Evaluator for unbiased objective estimation.
        \item MC-SAA Greedy Heuristic with IMRank candidate screening.
        \item Binary Evolutionary Optimization using pymoo with feasibility repair.
\end{itemize}

We implement experiments on provided and generated datasets, with detailed timing and score reporting for both heuristic and evolutionary pipelines. The report also includes a cross-machine performance analysis to understand hardware impacts on runtime. All algorithms and experiments are designed for reproducibility, with fixed random seeds and batch scripts for execution. See Github for complete scripts and data: \url{https://github.com/hotteano/Artificial-Intelligence-H-Course-Material-and-Notes-for-SPR26-in-SUSTech}.

\subsection{Related Work}

Information Exposure Maximization (IEM) was first formalized by Garimella et al.~\cite{garimella2018balancinginformationexposuresocial}, who studied how to reduce information disparity between opposing campaigns in social networks. Their work established the IC-based exposure balancing framework and showed that even simple greedy heuristics can be effective for this objective. However, they focused on unbiased Monte Carlo evaluation without exploring algorithmic optimizations for large-scale graphs.

For single-campaign Influence Maximization (IM), extensive research has developed efficient approximation algorithms. IMRank~\cite{cheng2014imrankinfluencemaximizationfinding} provides a fast ranking-based method for candidate screening, which we adapt for the IEM context to reduce marginal gain evaluation costs. While IMRank was originally designed for single-campaign influence spread, our work demonstrates its effectiveness for dual-campaign exposure balancing.

Simulated Annealing (SA) has been explored for influence maximization problems. Wu et al.~\cite{wu2022budgetedinfluencemaximizationboost} proposed a boost SA approach for budgeted influence maximization, showing that SA can escape local optima better than greedy methods when the objective landscape is rugged. Our work extends this insight to the IEM setting, combining GA global exploration with SA local refinement, and introducing an adaptive mechanism to disable SA for dense graphs where its overhead outweighs benefits.

\section{Method}

In this section, we introduce the three core algorithms in our IEM pipeline: the Monte Carlo IC Evaluator, the MC-SAA Greedy Heuristic with IMRank Screening, and the Binary Evolutionary Optimization using Genetic Algorithm. Each algorithm is designed to balance solution quality and computational efficiency under the constraints of the IEM problem.

\subsection{Algorithm A: Monte Carlo IC Evaluator}

Computing the IEM objective $\Phi$ exactly is \#P-hard due to the combinatorial nature of influence spread under the IC model. Therefore, we use a Monte Carlo (MC) simulation approach to estimate $\mathbb{E}[\Phi]$ for given seed sets $S_1$ and $S_2$.

For each simulation, both campaigns diffuse independently under IC. A node is considered reached if it is activated or attempted. The score of one simulation is $|V|-|R_1\oplus R_2|$, and the final estimate is the sample mean over $T$ runs:
\begin{equation}
\hat{\Phi}(S_1,S_2)=\frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^{T}\left(|V|-|R_1^{(t)}\oplus R_2^{(t)}|\right).
\end{equation}

See \ref{alg:eval} for the detailed algorithm. The computational complexity of this evaluator is $O(T \cdot \text{diffusion\_cost})$, where $\text{diffusion\_cost}$ depends on the graph structure and propagation probabilities. In practice, we find that $T=5000$ provides a good balance between estimation accuracy and runtime for our test cases.

\textbf{High-Probability Special Case ($p=0.99$).} When the propagation probability approaches 1.0 (e.g., $p=0.99$), the diffusion process becomes nearly deterministic: almost all reachable nodes will be activated in a single cascade. In this \textit{trivial propagation} scenario, Monte Carlo simulation is unnecessary because the outcome has negligible variance. The evaluator implements an optimization that directly computes the reachable set via graph traversal (BFS/DFS) without repeated sampling, returning the exact exposure count in a single iteration. This optimization significantly reduces runtime for high-probability cases while producing results identical to Monte Carlo estimation with infinite simulations.
 
\begin{algorithm}[h]
\caption{Monte Carlo IC Evaluation}
\label{alg:eval}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Graph $G$, probabilities $(p_1,p_2)$, initial seeds $(I_1,I_2)$, additional seeds $(S_1,S_2)$, simulations $T$
\State $\text{sum}\gets 0$
\For{$t=1$ to $T$}
                \State Simulate IC for campaign 1 from $I_1\cup S_1$, get $R_1^{(t)}$
                \State Simulate IC for campaign 2 from $I_2\cup S_2$, get $R_2^{(t)}$
                \State $\text{sum}\gets \text{sum} + \big(|V|-|R_1^{(t)}\oplus R_2^{(t)}|\big)$
\EndFor
\State \Return $\text{sum}/T$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Algorithm B: MC-SAA Greedy with IMRank Screening}
The heuristic iteratively allocates one budget unit to either campaign 1 or campaign 2. In each step, it samples $N$ Monte Carlo base scenarios from current seeds. For each candidate node $v$, it estimates two marginal gains by incremental diffusion:
\begin{align}
h_1(v) &= \mathbb{E}\left[2\,|\Delta R_1(v)\cap R_2| - |\Delta R_1(v)|\right],\\
h_2(v) &= \mathbb{E}\left[2\,|\Delta R_2(v)\cap R_1| - |\Delta R_2(v)|\right].
\end{align}
Here $\Delta R_i(v)$ is the newly reached set when adding $v$ to campaign $i$ on top of a sampled base state. The better of $\max_v h_1(v)$ and $\max_v h_2(v)$ is selected.

To reduce candidate evaluation cost, IMRank \cite{cheng2014imrankinfluencemaximizationfinding} provides a ranked pool from both campaigns, then only top candidates are evaluated in each greedy step. Under graphs with higher probabilities, each Monte Carlo simulation tends to reach more nodes, which can make incremental diffusion more expensive. IMRanking helps focus on promising candidates, thus controlling runtime while maintaining solution quality.

\textbf{IMRank Implementation.} IMRank is an influence ranking algorithm that estimates node importance based on the sum of in-degrees from its neighbors, weighted by edge probabilities. The rank score for each node is computed iteratively until convergence:
\begin{equation}
r(v) = \sum_{u \in N^{in}(v)} p(u,v) \cdot \left(1 + r(u)\right),
\end{equation}
where $N^{in}(v)$ is the set of in-neighbors of $v$, and $p(u,v)$ is the propagation probability from $u$ to $v$. The algorithm performs the following steps:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Initialization:} Initialize all node ranks $r_0(v) = 1$ for all $v \in V$.
    \item \textbf{Iterative Update:} For each iteration $t = 1, 2, \ldots, T_{max}$:
    \begin{equation}
    r_t(v) = \sum_{u \in N^{in}(v)} p(u,v) \cdot \left(1 + r_{t-1}(u)\right).
    \end{equation}
    \item \textbf{Convergence Check:} Stop when $\|r_t - r_{t-1}\|_\infty < \epsilon$ (typically $\epsilon = 10^{-6}$) or $t = T_{max}$ (typically $T_{max} = 20$).
\end{enumerate}

In our implementation, we compute separate IMRank lists $L_1$ and $L_2$ for the two campaigns by running the algorithm on the respective edge probability sets. The top-$C$ nodes from each list form the candidate pool for greedy selection, reducing the number of expensive incremental diffusion evaluations from $|V|$ to $O(C)$ per step.

\begin{algorithm}[h]
\caption{IMRank Influence Ranking}
\label{alg:imrank}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Graph $G=(V,E)$, edge probabilities $p: E \to [0,1]$, max iterations $T_{max}$, tolerance $\epsilon$
\Ensure Ranked list of nodes $L$ sorted by influence score
\State Initialize $r_0(v) \gets 1$ for all $v \in V$
\For{$t = 1$ to $T_{max}$}
    \ForAll{$v \in V$}
        \State $r_t(v) \gets \sum_{u \in N^{in}(v)} p(u,v) \cdot (1 + r_{t-1}(u))$
    \EndFor
    \If{$\|r_t - r_{t-1}\|_\infty < \epsilon$}
        \State \textbf{break} \Comment{Convergence achieved}
    \EndIf
\EndFor
\State Sort nodes by $r_T(v)$ in descending order to obtain $L$
\State \Return $L$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

See \ref{alg:heu} for the detailed MC-SAA Greedy algorithm that utilizes IMRank for candidate screening.

The computational complexity of this heuristic is $O(k \cdot C \cdot N \cdot \text{diffusion\_cost})$, where $C$ is the candidate pool size and $\text{diffusion\_cost}$ depends on graph structure and probabilities. To be specific, diffusion cost of one incremental evaluation is $O(|E|)$ in worst case, but often much less in practice due to early termination when no new nodes are reached. In graph with higher transition probability, the cost will get higher. We test this property by experiment in the heuristic part, map5, which is a generated case designed to have higher probability and community structure. By tuning $C$ and $N$, we can balance runtime and solution quality effectively.

\begin{algorithm}[h]
\caption{MC-SAA Greedy with IMRank Candidate Pool}
\label{alg:heu}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Budget $k$, scenarios per step $N$, candidate pool size $C$
\State $S_1\gets\varnothing$, $S_2\gets\varnothing$, $U\gets V\setminus(I_1\cup I_2)$
\State Compute IMRank lists $L_1,L_2$ (optional if $C>0$)
\For{$t=1$ to $\min(k,|U|)$}
                \State Build candidate pool $P\subseteq U$ from top nodes of $L_1,L_2$ (or $P=U$)
                \State Sample $N$ base IC states for current $(I_1\cup S_1, I_2\cup S_2)$
                \ForAll{$v\in P$}
                                \State Estimate $h_1(v)$ and $h_2(v)$ using incremental diffusion on same $N$ states
                \EndFor
                \State Let $v_1^*=\arg\max_{v\in P} h_1(v)$, $v_2^*=\arg\max_{v\in P} h_2(v)$
                \If{$h_1(v_1^*) \ge h_2(v_2^*)$} add $v_1^*$ to $S_1$ \Else add $v_2^*$ to $S_2$ \EndIf
                \State Remove selected node from $U$
\EndFor
\State \Return $(S_1,S_2)$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Algorithm C: Binary Evolutionary Optimization}
The evolutionary solver encodes solution as a binary chromosome
\begin{equation}
x=[x^{(1)}\,|\,x^{(2)}] \in \{0,1\}^{2n_a},
\end{equation}
where $n_a$ is the number of available nodes, and bits in each half indicate inclusion in $S_1$ or $S_2$. Fitness is coarse Monte Carlo score with a tiny budget-usage tie-break bonus; pymoo minimizes $F(x)=-\text{fitness}(x)$.

Feasibility is enforced by repair:
\begin{itemize}
        \item remove dual assignments ($x_i^{(1)}=x_i^{(2)}=1$),
        \item if selected count exceeds budget, randomly drop extras,
        \item if below budget, randomly fill free nodes to reach target.
\end{itemize}

The evolutionary method uses standard genetic operators (selection, crossover, mutation) combined with feasibility repair to maintain valid solutions throughout the optimization process. See \ref{alg:evo} for the detailed algorithm.

The computational complexity of the evolutionary method is $O(P \cdot G \cdot \text{fitness\_cost})$, where $P$ is population size, $G$ is generations, and $\text{fitness\_cost}$ is the time to evaluate one chromosome (dominated by Monte Carlo simulations).

\begin{algorithm}[h]
\caption{Binary GA for IEM}
\label{alg:evo}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Population size $P$, generations $G$, budget $k$
\State Initialize binary population $\mathcal{X}$
\For{$g=1$ to $G$}
                \State Repair each chromosome to satisfy disjointness and budget
                \State Evaluate coarse MC fitness for each chromosome
                \State Selection + two-point crossover + bit-flip mutation
                \State Repair offspring and form next generation
\EndFor
\State Take best repaired chromosome $x^*$
\State Decode $x^*$ to $(S_1,S_2)$ and run fine MC evaluation
\State \Return $(S_1,S_2)$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Discussion}
The three components provide a practical trade-off between optimization quality and runtime:
\begin{itemize}
        \item The MC evaluator is unbiased but computationally expensive, especially for large $|V|,|E|$ and high simulation counts.
        \item The heuristic uses scenario sharing plus incremental diffusion, which significantly reduces repeated computation and is effective for medium/large graphs (as demonstrated on graphs with up to 36742 nodes).
        \item The evolutionary method explores a wider combinational space and can discover non-greedy seed allocations, but its cost depends heavily on population size, generation count, and MC fidelity. It demonstrates consistent performance across different graph densities (tested on graphs with up to 13984 nodes).
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Scope of comparison.} It is important to emphasize that the Heuristic and Evolutionary methods were evaluated on different graph instances (see Tables in Section~\ref{sec:data_sources}). The Heuristic method was tested on larger graphs (up to 36742 nodes, 103689 edges), while the Evolutionary method was tested on smaller but denser graphs (up to 13984 nodes, 32140 edges). Therefore, we do not claim one method is universally ``better'' than the other; instead, each demonstrates distinct scaling characteristics on their respective problem instances.

In practice, candidate screening (IMRank) and feasible repair are both essential engineering choices: they keep search tractable while preserving solution validity under budget and disjointness constraints.

\begin{figure}[h]

Experiments were conducted on two different machines with distinct hardware and software configurations:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Old Machine}: Intel Core Ultra 7 258V (Lunar Lake, 8 cores, low-power focused), Python 3.10, older library versions
    \item \textbf{New Machine}: Intel Core i7-14650HX (Raptor Lake, 16 cores, high-performance), newer Python and library versions
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Key observations:}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Heuristic Algorithm}: The new machine shows consistent but modest improvements: all cases (map1--map5) are 1--8\% faster on the new machine. The i7-14650HX's higher core count and clock speeds provide modest benefits for the heuristic method, though the improvement is limited by the memory-intensive nature of incremental diffusion.

    \item \textbf{Evolutionary Algorithm}: The new machine shows significant speedups for most cases: map2--map7 are 23--32\% faster on the new machine, confirming that the i7-14650HX's superior hardware specifications benefit the population-based evolutionary search. Map1 is slower (18\%) on the new machine, likely due to its dense graph structure (avg\_degree=28) requiring more computational effort per fitness evaluation, which may interact differently with the hardware memory hierarchy between the two machines.

    \item \textbf{Evaluator (Monte Carlo)}: The new machine is \textbf{1.21--1.32x faster} for pure evaluation tasks, improving throughput compared to the old machine.

    \item \textbf{Score Consistency}: Final scores are nearly identical across machines (within MC variance), confirming algorithmic correctness regardless of hardware.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Analysis}: The performance comparison between machines reveals distinct patterns for each algorithm:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Heuristic method}: The modest but consistent speedups (1--8\%) on the new machine suggest that the memory-intensive nature of incremental diffusion limits the benefits of higher core count and clock speeds. The i7-14650HX's superior specifications provide only marginal improvements for this algorithm.
    \item \textbf{Evolutionary method}: The significant speedups (23--32\% for map2--map7) demonstrate that the population-based evolutionary search benefits substantially from the new machine's higher core count and clock speeds. The only exception is map1, which is 18\% slower, likely due to its dense graph structure (avg\_degree=28) requiring more computational effort per fitness evaluation, which may interact differently with the hardware memory hierarchy between the two machines.
    \item \textbf{Hardware-software interaction}: The consistent improvements across most cases suggest that the i7-14650HX's hardware advantages (16 cores vs 8 cores, higher clock speeds) translate well to compute-intensive iterative algorithms when the software configuration is properly optimized.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Conclusion}: The cross-machine analysis demonstrates that the i7-14650HX (New Machine) provides consistent performance improvements over the Ultra 7 258V (Old Machine): 1--8\% for the heuristic method, 23--32\% for most evolutionary cases, and 21--32\% for pure Monte Carlo evaluation. These results confirm that hardware upgrades (higher core count, clock speeds) translate to tangible performance gains for compute-intensive iterative algorithms. The only outlier is map1 in the evolutionary pipeline, which is slower due to its dense graph structure requiring more computational effort per fitness evaluation. For reproducibility, we recommend documenting: (1) complete hardware specifications (CPU architecture, core count, memory); (2) software environment (Python version, library versions); (3) algorithm parameters including random seeds; and (4) runtime measurement methodology, as all these factors can significantly affect observed performance.

\section{Conclusion}
This report presents a unified IEM pipeline with three core algorithms: Monte Carlo IC evaluation, MC-SAA greedy heuristic with IMRank screening, and pymoo-based binary evolutionary optimization. The formulation, pseudocode, and batch experiment protocol support reproducible evaluation across provided and generated datasets.

Experimental results demonstrate distinct scaling characteristics: the heuristic method achieves efficient runtimes (17--55s) on large graphs with up to 36742 nodes, with performance primarily governed by propagation probability and diffusion reach. The evolutionary method provides consistent performance (12--136s) across graphs with up to 13984 nodes. Cross-machine comparison shows the i7-14650HX achieves 1--8\% speedup for heuristic, 23--32\% for evolutionary (except map1), and 21--32\% for Monte Carlo evaluation compared to the Ultra 7 258V.

Importantly, the two optimization methods were evaluated on different graph instances due to pipeline design requirements, so direct comparison of absolute scores or runtimes is not meaningful. Each method shows viable performance within its tested domain: the heuristic excels at handling large-scale sparse graphs through scenario sharing and incremental diffusion, while the evolutionary approach provides robust optimization across varying graph densities through population-based search and adaptive local refinement.
